The 10-Minute Morning Routine That Keeps You Strong After 60

By the Purposeful Living team · Updated July 2026 · 7 min read

The hardest part of staying strong after 60 isn't the exercise itself — it's fitting it in before the day runs away with you. A long gym session is easy to skip. Ten minutes, done first thing, in your pyjamas, next to the bed you just got out of? That's a habit that actually sticks.

This routine is built for real bodies and real mornings. It's gentle on the joints, needs no equipment, and moves through the five things that matter most as we age: waking the spine, loosening the joints, building leg strength, training balance, and steadying the breath. You can do the whole thing in about ten minutes, and every move can be made easier or harder to match how you feel that day.

A gentle note: this is general guidance, not medical advice. If you have a heart condition, recent surgery, severe arthritis, dizziness, or a fall history, check with your doctor or a physiotherapist before starting — and always stop if something hurts sharply.

Why mornings work so well

After sleep, your joints are stiff and your muscles have been still for hours. Gentle movement first thing does three useful things: it lubricates the joints so the rest of your day feels looser, it raises your heart rate just enough to lift your mood, and — crucially — it gets the workout done before "later" quietly disappears. Consistency beats intensity every single time. Ten minutes you do every day will do far more for you than an hour you manage twice a month.

You are not trying to exhaust yourself. You are reminding your body, every morning, that it is still strong and still yours.

The routine, step by step

Do each move slowly and with control. Breathe out on the effort. If you can only manage half the repetitions today, that still counts. Have a sturdy chair or the edge of a countertop nearby for balance.

1. Seated spinal wake-up (1 minute)

Sit tall on the edge of your bed, feet flat on the floor. Roll your shoulders back five times, then gently turn your head left and right, and finally look up and down. Place your hands on your knees and arch and round your back slowly, like a cat, for five breaths. This eases overnight stiffness and tells your nervous system the day has begun.

2. Ankle and wrist circles (1 minute)

Still seated, lift one foot and draw five slow circles with the ankle in each direction, then swap. Do the same with your wrists. These small joints are easy to ignore, but keeping ankles mobile is one of the best things you can do to prevent trips and falls.

3. Sit-to-stands (2 minutes)

This is the single most valuable exercise on the list. From your seated position, stand up without using your hands if you can — then sit back down with control. Aim for 8 to 12 repetitions. The sit-to-stand builds the exact leg and hip strength you need to rise from a chair, a car, or the floor for years to come. If it's hard, push up from your thighs; if it's easy, slow the lowering phase to three seconds.

4. Standing heel raises (1 minute)

Stand behind your chair, hands resting lightly on the back for support. Rise onto the balls of your feet, hold for a second, and lower slowly. Repeat 12 to 15 times. This strengthens your calves and ankles — the muscles that catch you when you stumble.

5. Standing marches (1 minute)

Holding the chair, march on the spot, lifting each knee to a comfortable height. Keep it gentle and rhythmic for about a minute. This warms the whole body, works your hip flexors, and gives your heart a light, friendly nudge.

6. Balance practice (1 minute)

Balance is a skill you keep by practising it. Stand near your chair and lift one foot slightly off the floor, holding for as long as you comfortably can — even five seconds is a win. Swap feet. As you improve, try letting go of the chair with one hand. Never practise balance without something sturdy within arm's reach.

7. Wall push-aways (1 minute)

Stand an arm's length from a wall, place your palms flat against it at shoulder height, and slowly bend your elbows to bring your chest toward the wall, then press back. Do 8 to 12. This is a shoulder- and chest-friendly version of a push-up that keeps your upper body strong for lifting, carrying, and pushing.

8. Calming breath and reach (1 minute)

Finish standing tall. Breathe in as you raise both arms overhead, breathe out as you lower them. Repeat five times. End with your hands on your heart, feeling it beat a little stronger than when you woke. That's the point.

How to make it a habit

  • Anchor it to something you already do. "Right after I turn off the alarm" or "before my first cup of tea" works better than a vague plan to exercise.
  • Lay out anything you need the night before. If your mat and chair are ready, there's one less reason to skip.
  • Track it simply. A tick on a wall calendar is oddly powerful. Aim not to break the chain.
  • Forgive the misses. One skipped day is nothing. Two in a row is a pattern to gently interrupt tomorrow.

When to do more — and when to rest

Once ten minutes feels easy for a couple of weeks, add a second round of sit-to-stands, or hold your balance a little longer. On days you feel unwell, sore, or simply tired, do the seated portion only and call it a success. Movement should leave you feeling more capable, never wrecked. Mild muscle awareness the next day is normal; sharp joint pain is a signal to ease off and, if it lingers, to get it checked.

None of this requires you to become a different person. It just asks for ten honest minutes — the small, repeated deposit that keeps you climbing your own stairs, lifting your own groceries, and getting down to play with grandchildren for many years to come.

Retirement & Purpose

5 Questions to Answer Before You Retire

By the Purposeful Living team · Updated July 2026 · 8 min read

Most people plan for retirement by watching a single number: the balance in their savings account. But the people who retire happily rarely say it was the money alone that made the difference. They say it was clarity — knowing what they were retiring to, not just what they were retiring from.

Before you hand in your notice or set a date, sit down with these five questions. Answer them honestly, ideally on paper, and ideally with your partner if you have one. They cover the practical and the personal, because a good retirement needs both.

This article is for general guidance and is not financial or tax advice. Everyone's situation is different — before making irreversible decisions about pensions, benefits, or drawdown, speak with a qualified, independent financial adviser and, where relevant, a tax professional.

1. Do I actually have enough — and for how long?

This is the question everyone worries about, so start here and get it off your chest. The honest answer requires two lists: what you expect to spend each year in retirement, and what income you'll have coming in to meet it (pensions, government benefits, savings drawdown, part-time work, rental income).

A few grounding points:

  • Your spending will change shape, not just shrink. Commuting and work costs fall; travel, hobbies, and — later — healthcare often rise.
  • Plan for a long life. Many people underestimate how long retirement lasts. Planning to 90 or beyond is prudent, not pessimistic.
  • Account for inflation. Prices roughly double over 25 years at modest inflation, so a fixed income buys less each year.

If the numbers don't quite work, you have real levers: working a couple more years, delaying your state pension for a larger payout, downsizing your home, or trimming a few large recurring costs. Knowing this before you retire is far less stressful than discovering it after.

The goal isn't to retire with the most money. It's to retire with enough — and with a clear plan for what "enough" is doing for you.

2. What will I do with my Mondays?

Work quietly provides four things beyond a paycheck: structure, purpose, social contact, and identity. Remove it overnight and, for many people, all four vanish at once. That's why some new retirees feel unexpectedly flat a few months in — the holiday feeling fades and the empty calendar sets in.

Picture an ordinary Wednesday six months into retirement. What time do you get up? Who do you talk to? What are you working toward? If the honest answer is "I'm not sure," that's not a reason to keep working — it's a reason to start building the non-financial side of your plan now: a class, a volunteer role, a project, a standing coffee with friends. Purpose rarely shows up on its own; it's something you construct.

3. How will I stay connected to people?

Loneliness is one of the biggest and most overlooked risks in retirement, with real effects on both mental and physical health. When work ends, a whole network of daily, low-effort social contact can disappear almost without you noticing.

Ask yourself where your community will come from once colleagues are gone. Old friends need tending; new friendships take deliberate effort. Groups built around a shared activity — a walking group, a choir, a class, a faith community, a volunteering team — tend to create the easiest, most durable connections. Put at least one such thing in place before you retire, not after.

4. Is my health plan as solid as my money plan?

Your retirement is only as good as the health you have to enjoy it. Two areas deserve attention now:

  • Your habits. The strength, movement, and eating patterns you build in your early 60s largely determine how mobile and independent you'll be in your 80s.
  • Your coverage. Understand exactly what your healthcare costs and coverage will look like once you stop working, including any gap before other benefits begin. Unexpected medical costs are one of the most common shocks to a retirement budget.

A little planning here protects both your body and your budget.

5. What does a good day, and a good decade, actually look like to me?

This is the question people skip, and it may be the most important. Retirement is not one long weekend — it's potentially your third act, twenty or thirty years of life you get to design. What do you want it to mean? Is there something you always meant to learn, make, see, or give back? A skill to pass on, a place to live, a cause to serve, a family to be more present for?

You don't need a grand mission. You need a direction — a rough sense of what a well-lived version of these years would contain — so that your days add up to something you'll be glad about looking back.

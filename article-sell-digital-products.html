Digital Income

How to Create and Sell a Simple Digital Product After 60

By the Purposeful Living team · Updated July 2026 · 10 min read

This article may contain affiliate links. If you buy through them, we may earn a small commission at no extra cost to you. We only suggest things we'd recommend to a friend.

Trading hours for money has a hard ceiling: there are only so many hours, and after 60 you'd probably rather spend some of them on a walk than a workbench. A digital product breaks that ceiling. You make it once, and it can sell again and again — while you sleep, while you travel, while you do absolutely nothing at all.

A "digital product" simply means something useful that people download rather than receive in the post: a guide, an e-book, a printable planner, a set of templates, a recorded course, a collection of recipes or patterns. No inventory, no shipping, no stock room. This guide takes you from idea to first sale in plain English.

This is general guidance, not financial or tax advice. Money you earn from selling products is income — keep simple records and check how it affects your taxes and any benefits. When in doubt, ask a tax professional.

Step 1: Find an idea people actually want

The best digital products solve a specific, real problem for a specific group of people. The mistake beginners make is starting from "what would be fun to create" instead of "what does someone need."

Find your idea where three things overlap:

  • What you know — a skill, a subject, a process you've mastered.
  • What people ask you about — the questions friends, family, or former colleagues bring you.
  • What people already pay for — if similar products sell, that's proof of demand, not competition to fear.

Examples that suit later-life makers well: a printable garden planner, a beginner's guide to a craft you've done for decades, a family-recipe cookbook, a set of budgeting templates, a genealogy research checklist, a "downsizing your home" workbook, or a short course teaching a professional skill from your career.

You don't need a big audience to start. You need one clear problem and one clear solution that a few people will gladly pay for.

Step 2: Choose the simplest possible format

Match the format to the problem and to your own comfort:

  • A PDF guide or e-book — the easiest starting point. If you can write in a word processor, you can make one.
  • A printable — a planner, checklist, tracker, or template people print at home. Quick to make and endlessly re-sellable.
  • A recorded video course — a little more involved, but powerful for teaching a hands-on skill.
  • A template pack — spreadsheets, letters, forms, or designs people can reuse.

Start with a PDF or printable. You can always make something fancier once you've proven people will buy.

Step 3: Create it (you already have the tools)

You likely have everything you need already. Free and low-cost tools let you write, design attractive pages with ready-made templates, and export a polished PDF — no design training required. Keep your first product focused and genuinely helpful rather than long. A tight 20-page guide that solves the problem beats a rambling 100-page one every time. Have a friend read it and tell you honestly whether it delivers what the title promises.

Step 4: Set a fair price

Beginners almost always price too low. Remember you're charging for the result — the time and frustration your product saves — not for the number of pages. A well-made printable might sell for a modest amount; a focused guide or a course that teaches a valuable skill can command much more. Look at what comparable products charge, price in that range, and don't apologise for the value of your experience. You can always test a higher price later.

Step 5: Sell it without needing a website

You do not need to build a website to start. Dedicated selling platforms handle the storefront, the secure payment, and the automatic delivery of the file to your buyer — all for a small cut of each sale. You simply upload your product, set a price, and get a link you can share. That link is your shop.

Step 6: Get your first sales

  • Tell your existing circle first. Friends, family, former colleagues, and any community group tied to your topic are your warmest audience.
  • Be helpful in the places your buyers gather. Answer questions in relevant online groups and forums, and mention your product only where it genuinely helps.
  • Collect emails. Even a small list of interested people lets you tell them about this product and the next one.
  • Ask happy buyers for a review. Social proof sells the next copy for you.

Step 7: Do it again

The magic compounds. Once your first product is live and earning quietly in the background, a second one — often for the same audience — is far easier, because you already have buyers who trust you. Two or three small products can add up to a steady, pleasant stream of income that keeps arriving long after the work is done.
